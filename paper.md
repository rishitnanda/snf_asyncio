**Rishit Nanda**

## Abstract

SMT-based verification of concurrent programs produces encodings that routinely exhibit solver instability — wildly varying solve times and timeouts under semantics-preserving mutations — even when the underlying program logic is simple. We show that for Python asyncio programs satisfying a condition we call *controlled interference*, this instability is not fundamental: it is a direct consequence of encoding the scheduler's nondeterminism as a universally quantified formula rather than deriving the encoding from the scheduler's structural properties.

We introduce *Scheduling Normal Form* (SNF), a four-condition characterization of asyncio's event loop grounded in CPython 3.12.3 source code. We prove that any program satisfying SNF and *extended controlled interference* — shared state mediated exclusively through `asyncio.Queue`, `asyncio.Lock`, or `asyncio.Event`, plus unmediated read-only shared accesses (SNF-5) — admits an encoding derivable from program structure alone: a quantifier-free formula in QF_LIA whose disjunction size is bounded by the number of FIFO-consistent dispatch orderings, strictly fewer than the naive $n!$. We prove this encoding is complete with respect to feasible traces and free of all three root-cause instability classes identified in Cebeci's SMT instability taxonomy. A corpus study of 33 open-source asyncio repositories finds that 71.54% of shared-variable interactions in practice satisfy extended controlled interference (3.13% already mediated, 68.41% read-only). The remaining 28.46% decomposes into commutative writes (3.28%) and destructive write-conflicts (25.18%), both of which we name as open problems rather than claim to solve: the commutative case lacks a precise definition of commutativity over our abstract machine, and the write-conflict case is shown in Section 6 to break the theorem's proof at Lemma 2a.

Empirically, we identify two distinct phenomena. On seven asyncio benchmarks including b7 (read-only broadcast, validating the SNF-5 extension), naive AUFLIA encodings of three benchmarks return SAT on every run — incorrectly asserting the property is satisfiable — while structured QF_LIA encodings correctly return UNSAT on all seven. This correctness gap is the primary empirical finding. On PHP pigeonhole proxy benchmarks, naive AUFLIA encodings show standard deviation growing from 6ms to 24103ms; structured QF_LIA encodings remain flat at σ ≤ 0.9ms, with 11/50 timeouts on naive versus 0/50 on structured at PHP6. A non-SNF QF_LIA baseline (Big-OR/pairwise quantifier elimination without scheduling-structure knowledge, applied to b3 and PHP3–PHP6 — the benchmarks with genuine permutation symmetry) shows that quantifier elimination alone closes the stability gap: non-SNF σ ≤ 1.2ms, comparable to structured. The contribution of SNF is therefore structural derivability and completeness rather than additional variance reduction beyond the theory change from AUFLIA to QF_LIA. Structured encodings are stable on both Z3 and CVC5 (σ ≤ 0.9ms across all benchmarks and both solvers).

## 1. Introduction

Solver-aided verification tools — Dafny, Verus, Ivy, and custom SMT-based analyzers — routinely produce queries that behave erratically under Z3. The same query, subjected to semantics-preserving mutations (variable renaming, assertion reordering, seed injection), can complete in 5ms on one mutation and timeout after 60 seconds on another. Cebeci's SMT instability taxonomy, derived from detailed case studies of eleven unstable queries across four production verifiers, traces every case to one of three root causes: quantifier/relevancy mismatches in Z3's E-matching engine, misconfigured quantifier instantiation parameters, or misaligned trigger expectations. His diagnosis is empirical and post-hoc: given an unstable query, trace the instability to its cause and patch. No systematic method exists for designing encodings that avoid instability from the outset.

Concurrent programs present a particularly sharp version of this problem. Encoding nondeterministic scheduling — the combinatorial space of possible task dispatch orderings — typically requires either universal quantification over scheduling choices or explicit enumeration of all $n!$ orderings. The former produces exactly the quantified formula structure that Cebeci's taxonomy identifies as the root cause of instability classes A, B, and C. The latter grows super-exponentially with coroutine count and is impractical for more than a handful of tasks.

This paper proposes a third path. When the scheduler has enough structural regularity, its nondeterminism can be encoded as a finite, statically-derivable disjunction of ground formulas — quantifier-free by construction, stable by the argument that it never triggers the quantifier instantiation machinery that causes instability, and complete by the argument that the disjunction covers exactly the feasible traces.

Python's asyncio scheduler is a particularly clean instance of this idea. Its event loop is cooperative, single-threaded, FIFO-dispatching, and implemented in well-documented CPython source. We show these properties are sufficient to derive a provably stable encoding, instantiate this formally as Scheduling Normal Form, prove the resulting encoding is complete and quantifier-free, and validate both claims empirically.

The empirical work surfaces a finding we did not anticipate: naive encodings of three asyncio benchmarks are not merely unstable, they are wrong. They return SAT on properties that are genuinely UNSAT, because the naive encoding under-constrains the scheduler in a way that allows the solver to find a spurious satisfying assignment. This correctness gap is arguably the more important practical finding — a verifier built on the naive encoding would give false assurance.

### 1.1 Contributions

1. **Scheduling Normal Form (SNF):** four conditions on a cooperative async scheduler, each grounded in CPython 3.12.3 source, sufficient to derive a stable SMT encoding from program structure alone.

2. **Normal Form Reduction Lemma:** the feasible execution traces of any SNF-satisfying program under extended controlled interference are in bijection with FIFO-and-rendezvous-consistent dispatch permutation sequences — finite and statically enumerable, without any NP-hard oracle.

3. **Main theorem:** the derived encoding $\text{Enc}(P)$ is quantifier-free, complete with respect to feasible traces, and free of all three root-cause instability classes in Cebeci's taxonomy.

4. **SNF-5 (read-only sharing), proved:** extended controlled interference admits unmediated read-read shared accesses at zero extra encoding cost (Proposition 1a); Lemma 2a and the bijection of Claim 2 are shown to be unaffected by such accesses.

5. **Real-world corpus study:** an AST-based scan of 33 open-source asyncio repositories shows 71.54% of shared-variable interactions satisfy extended controlled interference in practice (3.13% mediated, 68.41% read-only under SNF-5).

6. **Trace-equivalence for commutative writes (partial):** we define trace-equivalence — invariance of every observable intermediate value, not just final state — and prove a sufficient condition (disjoint-key writes with no aggregate read before completion, Proposition 10) that gives the same zero-encoding-cost treatment as SNF-5. This is a sufficient condition, not a full characterization, and we show explicitly why it correctly excludes counters.

7. **Static conflict checker (practical enforcement, not verification):** a conservative classifier (mediated / read-only / checker-cleared / flagged) with a soundness-of-detection proof (Proposition 11) — *sound* here means zero false negatives: no unsafe interaction is ever misclassified as safe, though the checker may over-flag some genuinely safe interactions as needing fallback verification (a false positive in the conservative direction, not a false negative) — so a verifier built on SNF can safely fall back to general-purpose verification on the 25.18% of interactions outside the theorem's scope rather than silently mis-verify them. Soundness of havoc-injection over-approximation is proved for the sub-case where the flagged variable does not gate a branch (Proposition 12), which covers 36.21% of the flagged bucket (9.1% of the full corpus) by direct measurement. For the majority sub-case where the flagged variable does gate a branch (63.79% of the flagged bucket, 16.1% of the full corpus), a path-sensitive generalization of the encoding (Proposition 13, tightened by Propositions 13a–13b) is now proved sound for the further sub-case where the branching is finite and statically enumerable (the Bounded-Path Assumption); by a syntactic proxy measurement, this covers 92.24% of the flagged-control bucket (58.84% of the whole flagged bucket), leaving 7.76% (4.95% of the flagged bucket) — flagged-control variables gating a genuinely unbounded loop — fully open.

8. **Eager-task-factory first-step split (resolved):** a formal case split (Definitions 8, Propositions 7–8) covering eager first steps that touch no shared state, extended by Proposition 8′ to remove that restriction entirely — the theorem now applies to `eager_task_factory` programs with no restriction beyond what it and the static checker already require of every coroutine's shared-state accesses. A corpus-wide scan additionally found zero of 33 repositories referencing `eager_task_factory` at all.

9. **uvloop Consistency Interface:** a four-property, implementation-agnostic interface (Definitions 9, Proposition 9) such that any scheduler satisfying it inherits SNF and everything built on it; we perform this check against uvloop 0.22.1's Cython source and confirm all four properties hold (Section 7), so uvloop inherits SNF and the main theorem without modification.

10. **Thread-Disjointness Condition (TDC):** a checkable side-condition (Definitions 17–18, Proposition 14) that replaces the prior blanket exclusion of asyncio programs mixing explicit threading with a graduated treatment identical to the existing unmediated-shared-write checker. Call-graph-based re-measurement across the corpus (Section 6) found three candidate overlaps, all confirmed benign or correctly synchronized on inspection — zero confirmed violations, motivating a revision to the guard clause to recognize `Event`/`Condition`/`Barrier` handoffs in addition to `Lock`/`RLock`.

11. **CPython version conformance via the Consistency Interface (Corollary 9a), checked:** the pre-3.12/3.13+ version-transfer question is reduced from an open re-derivation to a bounded check of I-1–I-4 against each version's source, reusing the interface already validated against `uvloop` and Trio. We ran this check against CPython 3.9.19–3.13.0 (Section 7): all five versions show every witness pattern present.

12. **Correctness gap finding:** naive AUFLIA encodings of three asyncio benchmarks return SAT incorrectly on 50/50 mutation-sweep runs; structured QF_LIA encodings return UNSAT correctly on all seven benchmarks including b7 (read-only broadcast, validating SNF-5).

11. **Non-SNF baseline result:** a QF_LIA encoding without SNF structure (Big-OR/pairwise applied to b3 and PHP3–PHP6) is also stable (σ ≤ 1.2ms), establishing that instability is a quantifier phenomenon and SNF's contribution is structural derivability and completeness rather than additional variance reduction.

12. **Cross-solver stability:** structured QF_LIA encodings show σ ≤ 0.9ms on Z3 and σ ≤ 0.5ms on CVC5 across all benchmarks.

13. **Real-world case study (Section 5.7):** a hand-derived structured encoding of `asyncpg`'s production connection-pool checkout logic verifies the real mutual-exclusion safety property (`unsat` on double-checkout across all 6 feasible orderings), and a constructed naive-encoding counterpart exhibits the same correctness-gap phenomenon as Section 5.2 on real code rather than a synthetic benchmark; the case study also identifies that SNF-4's coverage extends to `asyncio.LifoQueue` (used by `asyncpg.Pool`), since it overrides only item-storage methods, not the FIFO waiter-wakeup machinery SNF-4 depends on. An automated source-to-SMT pipeline (`snf-pipeline`, Section 5.7) reproduces the same naive/structured mechanism mechanically when given an explicit, disclosed assumption about `Pool.acquire()`'s external call pattern (which the hand-encoding made implicitly); run unmodified with no such assumption, the pipeline correctly reports no gap, since a single file cannot on its own prove how many concurrent callers a public method has. Extended corpus-wide: 22 of 70 real-world sync-object checks across the Section 5.6 corpus show the same naive/structured gap pattern.

14. **Scaling data beyond a single point (Section 5.4.1):** the structured encoding is measured out to $n=100$ (flat, sub-5ms), the non-SNF baseline is shown to become impractical to even construct around $n \approx 9$–$10$ due to raw $n!$ disjunct materialization, and a naive quantified encoding of the same benchmark family is shown *not* to reproduce instability — an honest negative result clarifying that quantifier-based instability requires specific combinatorial coupling (as in the PHP family), not merely the presence of a quantifier.

## 2. Background and Related Work

### 2.1 SMT Instability and Cebeci's Taxonomy

Mariposa is a benchmarking framework that measures SMT solver instability by applying semantics-preserving mutations to queries and recording solve-time variance and outcome changes under different seeds, variable renamings, and assertion orderings. Cebeci applies Mariposa to eleven unstable queries drawn from Dafny, Ivy, Verus, and TPot, and identifies three root-cause classes:

**Class A (Quantifier/relevancy instability).** Z3's CDCL(T) core uses a relevancy filter that marks ground terms in currently-irrelevant atoms as ineligible for trigger matching. Universal quantifiers interact with this filter to produce heuristic branching instability. When E-matching fails, Z3 invokes its Model-Based Quantifier Instantiation (MBQI) engine as a fallback, which introduces additional nondeterminism. Cebeci's `patch-mbqi` fix addresses this class directly.

**Class B (Misconfiguration).** Two cases trace to suboptimal quantifier instantiation parameters, concretely `smt.qi.eager_threshold`, which controls the cost threshold for eager quantifier instantiation. This parameter is inert on quantifier-free formulas.

**Class C (Misaligned trigger expectations).** Three cases (`issue_7444`, `IfNorm0`, `root0`) arise when users expect triggers — annotations on quantified formulas that direct E-matching — to fire on ground terms that relevancy filtering suppresses.

All eleven cases in Cebeci's study require quantifiers in the query. His contribution is diagnostic; ours is prescriptive. We prove that for asyncio programs satisfying SNF, the encoding derived from program structure never introduces quantifiers, structurally avoiding all three root causes.

### 2.2 Partial-Order Reduction

Herbreteau, Larroze-Jardiné, and Walukiewicz (CONCUR 2025) prove that optimal stateful partial-order reduction is NP-hard even for acyclic programs with only `await` instructions — a direct lower bound on what any scheduler-pruning approach can claim for the general case. SNF sidesteps this result by restricting to programs satisfying controlled interference, for which the permutation set $F_t$ is structurally bounded and enumerable without an NP-hard oracle. The restriction is not a convenience; Herbreteau et al.'s result explains why the general case cannot be handled polynomially and why a structural restriction is necessary.

Schemmel et al. integrate quasi-optimal partial-order reduction with KLEE for POSIX-threaded C programs. KLEE+POR defines independence by shared memory access rather than yield-point boundaries, targets exhaustive bug-finding for general multithreaded programs, and makes no claims about SMT query stability or solver behavior under mutation.

ESST (Explicit Scheduler, Symbolic Threads) combines explicit-state scheduler enumeration with symbolic per-thread execution for cooperative programs, targeting model checking of correctness properties. Its goal is interleaving coverage, not encoding stability; it does not address quantifier structure in generated queries.

### 2.3 Asyncio Verification

To our knowledge, no prior work derives provably stable SMT encodings for Python asyncio programs or connects asyncio's scheduler structure to Cebeci's instability taxonomy. This work is the first to identify a correctness gap in naive asyncio encodings — the phenomenon where under-constraining the scheduler produces spurious SAT results.

**Relation to prior work.** None of the ingredients above is individually new: quantifier-free encodings of concurrent programs go back to bounded model checking (Clarke et al. 2001), partial-order reduction is a decades-old field, the diagnosis that AUFLIA quantifiers cause Z3 instability is Cebeci's, and using scheduler structure to prune interleavings is ESST's central idea. What is new is the combination and its consequences: deriving a QF encoding directly from cooperative async program structure — encoding only structurally-feasible orderings rather than eliminating quantifiers from a naive encoding after the fact; grounding that derivation in CPython 3.12.3 source with line-level witnesses for all four SNF conditions; connecting the derivation to Cebeci's taxonomy as a forward-looking design criterion, turning his diagnostic methodology prescriptive for a specific program class; proving the derived encoding complete rather than merely stable; and, empirically, discovering and precisely characterizing the correctness gap — naive asyncio encodings do not merely exhibit timing instability, they answer the wrong question and return incorrect results on properties involving universal quantification over scheduling orders. The correctness gap was not anticipated going in and is, in practical terms, the stronger finding: a verifier built on the naive encoding would not just be slow or unreliable, it would assert safety for programs that are not safe under all feasible orderings.

## 3. Scheduling Normal Form

### 3.1 Abstract Machine

A *program* $P$ is a finite set of coroutines $C = \{c_1, \ldots, c_n\}$ together with a finite set of synchronization objects $\text{Sync}(P)$, each an instance of `asyncio.Queue`, `asyncio.Lock`, or `asyncio.Event`. A *coroutine* $c_i$ is a sequence of steps separated by *yield points* $y_{i,1}, y_{i,2}, \ldots$ at each of which $c_i$ either suspends awaiting a future or terminates. In CPython 3.12, every `await expr` compiles to a `SEND`/`YIELD_VALUE` bytecode pair — the concrete realisation of a yield point at an addressable bytecode offset.

The *ready queue* $Q \subseteq C$ is `BaseEventLoop._ready`, a FIFO deque of `Handle` objects. A *scheduler* $S : 2^C \to C$ maps a non-empty ready queue to the next coroutine to dispatch.

**Controlled interference.** Let $\text{Vars}(c_i)$ denote variables directly read or written by $c_i$, excluding accesses through objects in $\text{Sync}(P)$. Coroutines $c_i$ and $c_j$ are directly non-interfering if $\text{Vars}(c_i) \cap \text{Vars}(c_j) = \emptyset$. A program $P$ satisfies *controlled interference* if all coroutine pairs are directly non-interfering and all shared state is accessed exclusively through $\text{Sync}(P)$.

**Rendezvous point.** A *rendezvous point* is a pair $(p_i, g_j)$ where $c_i$ performs a put-side operation on some $s \in \text{Sync}(P)$ at yield point $y_{i,p}$ and $c_j$ performs the corresponding get-side operation at $y_{j,g}$. Resolution of the rendezvous establishes a happens-before relation $y_{i,p} \to y_{j,g}$.

**Extended controlled interference (SNF-5).** A variable $x$ satisfies the *read-only sharing condition* if at most one coroutine writes $x$ during any execution of $P$, and that coroutine writes $x$ only before any coroutine reads $x$ (write-once-before-read semantics). $P$ satisfies *extended controlled interference* if it satisfies controlled interference for all write-write and write-read accesses, and additionally satisfies the read-only sharing condition for any remaining unmediated shared accesses — i.e. unmediated read-read accesses, where multiple coroutines only read a shared variable that no coroutine ever writes, are permitted without mediation. This is a condition on the program's access patterns, not a fifth scheduler condition: it extends the controlled-interference precondition rather than adding to SNF-1–SNF-4.

**Proposition 1a (SNF-5 correctness).** The Normal Form Reduction Lemma and Propositions 2–4 below hold under extended controlled interference in place of controlled interference. *Proof.* For a variable $x$ satisfying read-only sharing, a read of $x$ never calls `put_nowait`, `release`, or `set()` — the only CPython operations triggering `_wakeup_next` — so Lemma 2a (below) is unaffected: whether any rendezvous resolves during step $t$ remains a function of $Q_t$ and the put-side coroutine's own execution alone. Since no coroutine writes $x$ during $P$'s execution, all coroutines reading $x$ observe the same value regardless of relative dispatch order, so Claim 2's bijection — which distinguishes traces by scheduling choice, not by observable state alone — is preserved. Read accesses never appear in $\text{Rendezvous}(t)$, so $F_t$ is unchanged and Proposition 4's completeness argument carries through unchanged. $\square$ Consequently no ordering constraint on read-only accesses needs to appear in $\Phi_t$, and $|F_t|$ remains bounded as in Proposition 5 — SNF-5 adds coverage at zero encoding cost.

### 3.2 The Four SNF Conditions

**SNF-1 (Determinism).** $S(Q)$ is a total function: identical $Q$ always yields the same dispatch choice.

*CPython witness (3.12.3).* `_run_once` captures `ntodo = len(self._ready)` before the dispatch loop and calls `_ready.popleft()` once per handle for `i in range(ntodo)` (`base_events.py` lines 61–63). Dispatch order equals insertion order; no randomness is introduced.

**SNF-2 (Bounded dispatch).** $|Q|$ at any dispatch step is bounded above by $|C|$.

*CPython witness (3.12.3).* Handles appended to `_ready` during the current iteration are not dispatched until the next `_run_once` call (the loop iterates `range(ntodo)`, not the live deque). Each coroutine contributes at most one `Handle` per iteration, giving `ntodo ≤ |C|`.

**SNF-3 (Yield-point atomicity).** Between two consecutive yield points of $c_i$, no other coroutine's state changes.

*CPython witness (3.12.3).* `Task.__step` calls `coro.send(None)` synchronously in `__step_run_and_handle_result` (`tasks.py`). The coroutine executes until `YIELD_VALUE`; only then does `__step` return and the loop can dispatch another task. The single-threaded event loop ensures no other coroutine is dispatched during a coroutine's step — this is the scheduling-level atomicity SNF-3 requires. The GIL additionally prevents concurrent mutation of Python objects across OS threads, but SNF-3 does not require this stronger property: it requires only that no other coroutine's state changes during $c_i$'s step, which holds by the cooperative single-threaded design of the event loop regardless of the GIL.

**SNF-4 (Rendezvous ordering).** For each synchronization object in $\text{Sync}(P)$, the wakeup order of waiting coroutines follows FIFO order of `await` arrival — a policy derivable statically from program structure.

*CPython witness (3.12.3).* `asyncio.Queue` maintains FIFO deques `_getters` and `_putters`; `_wakeup_next` calls `waiters.popleft()`. `asyncio.Lock` maintains FIFO `_waiters`; `_wake_up_first` calls `next(iter(self._waiters))`. `asyncio.Event.set()` resolves all waiters simultaneously; their relative dispatch order within the following iteration follows `_ready` FIFO insertion order, tracing back to `await` arrival order.

**Scope.** These witnesses are verified by direct inspection against CPython 3.12.3 with the default task factory. They do not directly cover `uvloop` and other alternative event loops, CPython versions prior to 3.12, or programs using `asyncio.eager_task_factory` — but none of these is a dead end: Section 7 shows `uvloop` inherits SNF via the Consistency Interface (Definitions 9, Proposition 9); Corollary 9a routes pre-3.12/3.13+ version conformance through the same interface, checked against CPython 3.9–3.13 in Section 6; and Section 6 also resolves `eager_task_factory` directly via Propositions 7, 8, and 8′, removing the restriction entirely rather than working around it. The theorem's direct-inspection base case is `asyncio.BaseEventLoop` in CPython 3.12 with the default factory; its checked scope beyond that base case is summarized in the Status table at the end of the companion document.

**Proposition 1.** CPython's `asyncio.BaseEventLoop` (3.12.3, default task factory) satisfies SNF-1 through SNF-4. *Proof by direct inspection of cited source locations.* $\square$

### 3.3 Normal Form Reduction Lemma

**Lemma.** Let $P$ satisfy controlled interference with scheduler satisfying SNF, $|C| = n$, $T$ dispatch steps. The feasible execution traces of $P$ are in bijection with sequences $(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_T)$ where each $\sigma_t$ is a permutation of $Q_t$ consistent with FIFO order and the happens-before constraints from resolved rendezvous points.

*Proof sketch via three claims.*

**Claim 1 (Sequential determinism).** The segment between any two consecutive yield points of $c_i$ runs inside one `coro.send(None)` call. By SNF-3 no other coroutine executes during this call; the state of every $c_k \neq c_i$ is constant throughout. $\square$

**Claim 2 (Correspondence).** By SNF-1 each dispatch step produces a unique scheduling choice, so every trace maps to a unique permutation-sequence. Conversely, any FIFO-and-rendezvous-consistent permutation is reachable: for `sleep(0)`, insertion order into `_ready` traces deterministically to task creation order; for rendezvous points, SNF-4 establishes FIFO wakeup order. The bijection is over *sequences of scheduling choices* — two traces with identical final variable valuations but different dispatch sequences are distinct traces, distinguished by their permutation-sequence. For programs with synchronization, dispatch order affects observable state (different Queue orderings produce different item-delivery sequences); for directly non-interfering programs with no synchronization, the encoding verifies scheduling properties only. $\square$

**Lemma 2a (Resolution-set invariance).** For any dispatch step $t$ and any coroutine $c_j$ awaiting a rendezvous, whether $c_j$'s future resolves during step $t$ depends only on which coroutines execute during $t$ and their own local execution — not on the relative dispatch order of those coroutines within $Q_t$. *Proof.* Follows from Claim 1 and SNF-3: by Claim 1 each coroutine's execution between yield points is fixed independent of scheduling order, and by SNF-3 the put-side coroutine's atomic segment excludes execution by any other coroutine, so whether `_wakeup_next` fires within that segment cannot depend on $c_j$'s or any other coroutine's position in $Q_t$. $\square$

**Claim 3 (Inductive composition).** By induction on $T$. $Q_{T+1}$ is determined by which coroutines completed their awaited futures during step $T$. Under controlled interference, non-interfering segments resolve independently by Claim 1, and rendezvous resolution is order-independent by Lemma 2a; hence $Q_{T+1}$ is invariant across FIFO-consistent permutations of $Q_T$. Extension by Claim 2 gives the bijection for $T+1$ steps. $\square$

## 4. The Encoding and Its Properties

### 4.1 General Encoding Enc(P)

For each coroutine $c_i$ and yield point $y_{i,j}$, introduce state variables recording $\text{Vars}(c_i)$ immediately after $y_{i,j}$. For each rendezvous point, introduce a ground ordering assertion derived from SNF-4. For each dispatch step $t$, let $F_t$ be the set of FIFO-and-rendezvous-consistent permutations of $Q_t$. The per-step formula is:

$$\Phi_t = \bigvee_{\sigma \in F_t} \left( \text{order}_t = \sigma \;\wedge\; \bigwedge_{c_i} \text{post\_constraints}(c_i, \sigma, t) \;\wedge\; \bigwedge_{r} \text{rendezvous\_constraint}(r, \sigma) \right)$$

where post\_constraints and rendezvous\_constraint are ground formulas in QF\_LIA. The full encoding is $\text{Enc}(P) = \Phi_\text{init} \wedge \Phi_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \Phi_k$.

**Worked example.** For the two-coroutine program:

```python
async def a():          async def b():
    x = 1                   y = 1
    await sleep(0)          await sleep(0)
    x = 2                   y = 2
```

The SNF-derived encoding with both FIFO-consistent orderings retained for scheduler-agnostic verification:

```python
s.add(x_pre == 1, y_pre == 1)          # Φ_init
s.add(z3.Or(                           # Φ_1
    z3.And(order,         x_post == 2, y_post == 2),   # σ = (a,b)
    z3.And(z3.Not(order), y_post == 2, x_post == 2),   # σ = (b,a)
))
```

`z3.is_quantifier` returns `False` on all assertions. Z3 returns `sat` for `x_post=2 ∧ y_post=2` and `unsat` for `x_post≠2 ∨ y_post≠2`. The mechanical verification script (`snf_encoding_experiment.py`) witnesses these three facts.

This example has a single dispatch step, no rendezvous points, and directly non-interfering coroutines — it cannot witness Claim 3 or Lemma 2a, both of which concern composition across steps and invariance across rendezvous resolution. The following example is constructed to exercise both.

**Worked example 2 (chained rendezvous).** Four coroutines, two chained rendezvous points sharing a coroutine, and an unrelated coroutine present to make invariance across dispatch orderings a nontrivial claim rather than a vacuous one:

```python
async def c0():               async def c1():
    q1.put_nowait("a")            x = await q1.get()
                                   await sleep(0)
                                   q2.put_nowait(x + "b")

async def c2():               async def c3():
    y = await q2.get()            z = 1
                                   await sleep(0)
                                   z = 2
```

`q1`, `q2` are `asyncio.Queue` instances. The rendezvous chain is $c_0 \to c_1 \to c_2$: $c_0$'s put on `q1` resolves $c_1$'s get (rendezvous $r_1$), and $c_1$'s subsequent put on `q2` resolves $c_2$'s get (rendezvous $r_2$), with $c_1$ shared between both constraints. The explicit `await sleep(0)` between $c_1$'s get and put splits $c_1$'s execution across two dispatch steps, so $r_2$ resolves one step after $r_1$ rather than within the same step. $c_3$ shares no synchronization object with $c_0$, $c_1$, or $c_2$ and is present in $Q_1$ alongside them, varying the dispatch order without participating in either rendezvous.

At step 1, $Q_1 = \{c_0, c_1, c_2, c_3\}$. $F_1$ consists of every FIFO-consistent permutation of $Q_1$ with $c_0$ before $c_1$ (required for $r_1$ to resolve within the step) — by Proposition 5's key lemma, this halves $S_4$ to 12 permutations, with $c_2$ and $c_3$ free to vary. The per-step encoding is:

$$\Phi_1 = \bigvee_{\sigma \in F_1} \left( \text{order}_1 = \sigma \;\wedge\; \text{rendezvous\_constraint}(r_1, \sigma) \right)$$

Two representative disjuncts, for $\sigma = (c_0, c_3, c_1, c_2)$ and $\sigma' = (c_3, c_0, c_1, c_2)$ — both in $F_1$, differing only in $c_3$'s position:

```python
s.add(z3.Or(
    z3.And(order_1 == (c0,c3,c1,c2), q1_order == put_before_get),   # σ
    z3.And(order_1 == (c3,c0,c1,c2), q1_order == put_before_get),   # σ'
    # ... remaining 10 disjuncts in F_1
))
```

By Lemma 2a, both disjuncts agree on which rendezvous resolve during step 1: $r_1$ resolves under both, since by SNF-3 $c_0$'s single-step body executes atomically once dispatched regardless of $c_3$'s position. $r_2$ does not resolve under either — the explicit `await sleep(0)` between $c_1$'s `q1.get()` and `q2.put_nowait()` forces $c_1$'s put to occur in a separate atomic segment from its get, so $c_1$ re-enters `_ready` at the end of step 1 without having executed the put. $Q_2 = \{c_1, c_3\}$ is identical whether $\sigma$ or $\sigma'$ was chosen — exactly Lemma 2a's invariance claim. ($c_2$ is not a member of $Q_2$: it is blocked on $q_2$'s getters queue, registered as a waiter rather than dispatched, until $r_2$ resolves at step 2.)

At step 2, $Q_2 = \{c_1, c_3\}$. $c_1$ resumes and executes `q2.put_nowait(x + "b")`, resolving $r_2$ and waking $c_2$ — but per SNF-2, a coroutine woken during step $t$ is not itself dispatched until step $t+1$, so $c_2$ does not join $Q_2$. $F_2$ consists of both FIFO-consistent permutations of $Q_2 = \{c_1, c_3\}$: since $c_1$ and $c_3$ do not interact, either order is feasible, and by Lemma 2a, whether $r_2$ resolves during step 2 depends only on $c_1$'s own dispatch, not on any ordering against $c_2$, which is not a member of $Q_2$ to begin with. $\Phi_2$ over $Q_2$ carries $r_2$'s rendezvous constraint:

$$\Phi_2 = \bigvee_{\tau \in F_2} \left( \text{order}_2 = \tau \;\wedge\; \text{rendezvous\_constraint}(r_2, \tau) \;\wedge\; \bigwedge_{c_i} \text{post\_constraints}(c_i, \tau, 2) \right)$$

$Q_3 = \{c_2\}$ (or $\{c_2, c_3\}$, depending on $c_3$'s step-2 position) is again invariant across $F_2$'s permutations by Lemma 2a, giving $\Phi_3$ over the remaining independent post-conditions.

*What this example tests.* The chain $c_0 \to c_1 \to c_2$ is the dependent-constraint case from Proposition 5, and $c_3$'s free-varying presence is what makes Lemma 2a's invariance claim nonvacuous: without $c_3$, $F_1$ would only vary over $\{c_0, c_1, c_2\}$, all of which trivially resolve $r_1$ in the same relative position. With $c_3$ present, the example is a genuine witness of Lemma 2a at step 1. The intervening `await sleep(0)` in $c_1$ additionally makes this a genuine witness of cross-dispatch-step rendezvous composition (Claim 3): $r_1$ resolves at step 1 and $r_2$ resolves at step 2, with $Q_2$'s invariance across step-1 orderings (Lemma 2a) feeding into $\Phi_2$'s rendezvous constraint for $r_2$ exactly as Claim 3's inductive step requires.

### 4.2 Propositions

**Proposition 2 (Size bound).** $|\text{Enc}(P)| = O(k \cdot |F_t| \cdot n)$ where $|F_t| \leq n!$. For $r$ independent rendezvous constraints, $|F_t| \leq n!/2^r$ by a transposition bijection argument: each happens-before constraint $c_i \to c_j$ eliminates exactly the $n!/2$ permutations in which $c_j$ precedes $c_i$, and independent constraints compound multiplicatively. For typical asyncio programs $n$ is in single digits and $r \geq 1$, making the actual $|F_t|$ far smaller than $n!$.

**Proposition 3 (Quantifier-freedom).** Every formula in $\text{Enc}(P)$ is in QF\_LIA. *Proof:* each $\Phi_t$ is a disjunction of conjunctions of ground constraints; $\Phi_\text{init}$ is a conjunction of ground equalities; $\text{Enc}(P)$ is quantifier-free by construction. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Completeness).** $\text{Enc}(P)$ captures all feasible execution traces of $P$ and no infeasible ones.

*Soundness:* every disjunct in $\Phi_t$ corresponds to a FIFO-and-rendezvous-consistent permutation, which by Claim 2 corresponds to a feasible trace. No infeasible ordering is represented.

*Completeness:* by Claim 2, every feasible trace corresponds to exactly one permutation-sequence; $F_t$ is the full set of consistent permutations at step $t$, so every permutation appearing in any feasible trace's sequence appears as a disjunct in $\Phi_t$. No feasible trace is excluded. $\square$

*Significance.* Without completeness, stability would come at the cost of missed behaviors. Proposition 4 guarantees that a verifier built on $\text{Enc}(P)$ will find all bugs that manifest on any feasible trace.

**Proposition 5 (Separation from naive QE).** For all programs with $r \geq 1$ independent rendezvous constraints per dispatch step, $|F_t| = n!/2^r < n!$. Standard quantifier elimination applied to the naive AUFLIA encoding either collapses to an unsatisfiable ground formula when the quantified space is provably empty by counting (as in the pigeonhole case), or produces $n!$ disjuncts per step without exploiting scheduling infeasibility. $\text{Enc}(P)$ differs in two ways: it is derived from program structure rather than produced by mechanical QE, and it is complete — it covers exactly the feasible traces and no infeasible ones (Proposition 4).

*Empirical remark.* SNF's disjunction-size reduction ($n!/2^r$ vs $n!$) does not produce measurable additional stability beyond the theory change from AUFLIA to QF\_LIA. We verified this with a non-SNF QF\_LIA baseline using Big-OR/pairwise quantifier elimination (no scheduling-structure knowledge, $n!$ disjuncts) on b3 and PHP3–PHP6 — the only benchmarks with genuine permutation symmetry. This baseline shows σ ≤ 1.2ms, comparable to the structured encoding's σ ≤ 0.9ms; both are flat against naive AUFLIA's σ up to 24103ms. The primary separation between $\text{Enc}(P)$ and naive QE is therefore correctness and completeness: the non-SNF baseline, while stable, requires additional correctness arguments to establish that it covers exactly the feasible traces. SNF provides both structural derivability and completeness by construction.

### 4.3 Main Theorem

**Theorem.** Let $P$ satisfy extended controlled interference with scheduler satisfying SNF (CPython 3.12.3, default task factory). $\text{Enc}(P)$ is: (1) quantifier-free (Proposition 3); (2) complete (Proposition 4); (3) free of root-cause instability classes A, B, and C in Cebeci's taxonomy. Extended controlled interference (Section 3.1, Proposition 1a) additionally admits unmediated read-only shared accesses beyond the mediated sharing controlled interference alone requires.

**Part A.** Class A requires a quantifier in the query — instability arises from E-matching and relevancy filtering on universal quantifiers, plus the MBQI fallback engine. By Proposition 3, $\text{Enc}(P)$ is quantifier-free; neither E-matching nor MBQI is ever invoked. The structural precondition for class A is absent. $\square$

**Part B.** Class B cases involve `smt.qi.eager_threshold`, a quantifier instantiation parameter. Since $\text{Enc}(P)$ contains no quantifiers, quantifier instantiation is never invoked regardless of this setting. Corollary of Part A. $\square$

**Part C.** Class C cases (`issue_7444`, `IfNorm0`, `root0`) involve trigger annotations on quantified formulas interacting badly with relevancy filtering. A quantifier-free formula carries no trigger annotations and never consults E-matching. Corollary of Part A. $\square$

## 5. Empirical Evaluation

### 5.1 Methodology

We implement a Mariposa-style mutation sweep (`mutate_sweep.py`). For each benchmark: 50 runs, distinct seeds per run, mutations applied in rotation — seed injection only, variable renaming only, both combined. Mutations are semantics-preserving by construction: renaming applies fresh names to all declared identifiers; assertion shuffling excludes domain-bound constraints (those of the form `(assert (>= x 0))`) that must maintain declaration order. A warmup run precedes each sweep to eliminate cold-start cache noise.

The solver is Z3 4.16.0 for the primary sweep; CVC5 is used for a cross-solver comparison on structured encodings only. Timeout is 60 seconds. All experiments run on CPython 3.12.3. Raw results are stored as per-run JSON records with seed, mutation flags, elapsed time in milliseconds, solver result, and timeout flag.

Two encodings are compared. The *naive encoding* uses `(set-logic AUFLIA)` with universal quantifiers over scheduling choices — the encoding a standard symbolic executor would produce without scheduler-structure knowledge. The *structured encoding* uses `(set-logic QF_LIA)` with finite disjunctions derived from SNF, no quantifiers.

### 5.2 Why Naive Returns SAT on Three Benchmarks

The correctness gap requires a precise explanation because it is not a solver error — it is a modeling error in the naive encoding.

The property in question for each of b2 (fire-forget cancellation), b4 (event broadcast wake guarantee), and b5 (race outcome) requires that the property holds *for all* feasible scheduling orders. The naive encoding asserts the existence of a scheduling order under which certain variable assignments are consistent — it encodes the scheduler's nondeterminism as an existential claim. For properties that hold universally across all orderings (b1, b3, b6), this distinction does not matter: if the property holds for all orderings then it holds for the particular ordering the solver finds, so both encodings return UNSAT on the negated property. But for properties that hold in some orderings and not others, the naive encoding finds the favorable ordering and returns SAT, while the structured encoding, which enumerates all feasible orderings explicitly as a disjunction, contains the ordering in which the property fails and correctly returns UNSAT.

b7 (read-only broadcast) does not exhibit a correctness gap because the property — all readers observe the broadcast value — holds universally across all FIFO-consistent orderings, so both the existential naive encoding and the universal structured encoding return UNSAT.

Concretely: b4 asks whether all coroutines waiting on an `asyncio.Event` are guaranteed to wake after `event.set()`. The naive encoding quantifies over wake orders and finds an ordering in which all waiters wake — returning SAT (the property *can* hold). The structured encoding encodes the constraint that *every* FIFO-consistent wakeup ordering must satisfy the property, identifies that the naive model admits impossible orderings, and returns UNSAT on the negated query (the property *must* hold under all feasible orders).

This is not a timing difference between encodings. It is the naive encoding answering a weaker question.

### 5.4 PHP Pigeonhole Proxy Benchmarks

The asyncio benchmarks are too small to drive the bimodal solve-time distributions characteristic of MBQI-level instability. PHP($n$): $n+1$ pigeons, $n$ holes, proven UNSAT by the pigeonhole principle. We use PHP3–PHP6 as a proxy for the structural scaling behavior of naive scheduler encodings.

**The structural connection.** The naive encoding of an asyncio program with $n$ coroutines universally quantifies over $n!$ scheduling permutations. PHP($n$) with naive AUFLIA encoding universally quantifies over assignment variables across $n!$-scale constraint combinations. Both query structures engage MBQI at scale for the same reason: the quantifier over combinatorially many cases exceeds E-matching's capacity, forcing MBQI fallback with heuristic instantiation. The structured QF\_LIA encoding for both problems replaces universal quantification with an explicit finite disjunction whose size is bounded by structural constraints — FIFO ordering for asyncio, the pigeonhole constraint for PHP — and never engages MBQI.

This is a proxy for query structure, not a model of asyncio programs. The PHP benchmarks demonstrate that the instability mechanism is real and grows with problem size; they do not predict exact timing numbers for large asyncio programs.

### 5.4.1 Scaling Beyond a Single Point: Where Does Each Encoding Become Impractical?

Table 2 reports disjunct counts and solve times at a single scale ($n=10$ for the mutex family, up to PHP6 for the pigeonhole family). This does not establish *how* each encoding scales as $n$ grows, or at what $n$ each becomes impractical — a gap distinct from the stability question already addressed. We extend the b3-mutex family (Section 5.4) across $n \in \{2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 15, 20, 30, 50, 100\}$ for the structured encoding, and $n \in \{2, 4, 6, 7, 8, 9\}$ for the non-SNF raw-permutation baseline, measuring both Z3 solve time and, for the non-SNF case, wall-clock time to *construct* the formula in the host language.

**Structured encoding scales linearly, not factorially.** Because all $n$ coroutines in this benchmark family execute symmetric code guarded by a single lock, the structured encoding's disjunct count is $n$ (one ground disjunct per possible last-acquirer outcome), not the $n!/2^r$ upper bound of Proposition 2 — confirming that bound is a worst case, not a typical case, for benchmarks with this kind of outcome symmetry. Measured solve time stays under 5ms out to $n=100$ (mean 4.16ms, $n=100$), while $n!$ itself exceeds $10^{18}$ by $n=20$. The structured encoding does not become impractical anywhere in this range.

**The non-SNF baseline's bottleneck is construction, not solving.** Materializing all $n!$ ground disjuncts in the host language — the honest cost of "brute-force disjunction" when the $n!/2^r$ upper bound is not reduced by symmetry, as it is not for the non-SNF baseline by construction — took 0.0s at $n=6$, 0.3s at $n=7$, 2.1s at $n=8$, and 18.9s at $n=9$ (362,880 disjuncts) for combined construction and solve; $n=10$ (3,628,800 disjuncts) was not attempted, since the growth trend indicates minutes rather than seconds. **The non-SNF baseline becomes impractical around $n \approx 9$–$10$ for benchmarks of this shape** — a concrete answer to "at what $n$" that Table 2's single data point ($n=10$, already at 470 disjuncts for a benchmark where symmetry happened to reduce the raw count) did not by itself establish.

**A naive quantified encoding of this same property did not reproduce instability.** We also built a naive AUFLIA-style encoding of the mutex benchmark using an uninterpreted order function and universal quantification, matching the paper's stated pattern of quantifying over scheduling choice. It solved in under 1ms out to $n=10$ without invoking MBQI. This is a genuine negative result, not a discarded inconvenient one: it demonstrates that quantifier-based instability, as Cebeci's taxonomy documents and Section 5.4's PHP results confirm, requires a specific kind of combinatorial coupling between the quantified variable and the constraint structure — not merely the presence of a universal quantifier. The mutex property here ("the final value is one of the $n$ ids") is well-matched to E-matching's triggers and does not force MBQI fallback, whereas the pigeonhole constraint does. This is consistent with, and strengthens, the paper's existing observation (Section 5.3) that b1, b3, b6, and b7 show no correctness gap because their properties hold universally and are structurally easy — scaling this same easy case to large $n$ does not manufacture instability, and only the PHP family (Section 5.4) is shown here or previously to exhibit it. We did not extend the PHP family beyond PHP6 for this experiment, since faithfully reproducing its exact naive-AUFLIA generation would require the original benchmark-generation script rather than a reconstruction; identifying PHP's precise breakdown point beyond PHP6's 11/50 timeouts is left as a direct extension of existing infrastructure rather than new methodology.

### 5.6 Real-World Corpus Study: How Much of Asyncio Satisfies (Extended) Controlled Interference?

Repositories were selected from the most-starred, actively-maintained repositories (>1k stars, commit activity within the last 12 months) spanning the major asyncio use-case categories — web serving, database access, task queuing, and low-level I/O — to avoid skewing toward any single domain.

The theorem's precondition — controlled interference, extended to SNF-5 — is only useful in practice if a meaningful fraction of real asyncio code satisfies it. We scan every shared-variable interaction (accesses to a variable shared between coroutines, whether or not it passes through `asyncio.Queue`, `asyncio.Lock`, or `asyncio.Event`) across 33 open-source asyncio repositories via AST-based static analysis, and classify each interaction as mediated, read-only (SNF-5), commutative-looking, or destructive write-conflict. This is the second expansion of the corpus: an original 22-repo pass was extended with 10 additional repos plus a `fastapi_examples` entry, targeting the database/cache-driver and task/queue categories, which were thinnest and most extreme on the smaller sample; `dramatiq` was dropped as statistically vacuous (1 interaction). We additionally re-ran the scan restricted to the actual async submodules of `sqlalchemy` (`lib/sqlalchemy/ext/asyncio` and the async dialect files) and `elasticsearch-py` (`elasticsearch/_async`), since both are mixed sync/async monorepos where an unscoped scan folds in sync-only code irrelevant to asyncio interference (see Section 6). All percentages below reflect this scoped rescan and are shares of the full 33-repo corpus (20,991 interactions) unless stated otherwise.

| Category | Share of corpus interactions | Status in this paper |
|---|---|---|
| Mediated (`Sync(P)`) | 3.13% | Covered by controlled interference (Section 3) |
| Read-only (SNF-5) | 68.41% | Covered by extended controlled interference (Proposition 1a) |
| Commutative writes | 3.28% | Trace-equivalence sufficient condition proved (Prop. 10, companion doc); corpus bucket not yet re-checked against it |
| Destructive write-conflicts | 25.18% | Detected soundly by static checker (Prop. 11); over-approximation soundness proved for flagged-data sub-case (Prop. 12, 36.21% of this bucket / 9.1% of corpus); flagged-control sub-case (63.79% of this bucket / 16.1% of corpus) now split further — Prop. 13 (tightened by 13a–13b) covers 92.24% of it (finite-branch, syntactic proxy measurement) with 7.76% (unbounded-loop-gated) still fully open |

The destructive bucket is deliberately conservative — any write to a variable also read by another coroutine is classified as destructive regardless of whether a runtime data-flow dependency actually exists; the true rate of genuine hazard is likely lower.

*A per-repository-category breakdown (mediated/read-only/commutative/destructive shares by domain — web frameworks, database/cache drivers, task/queue systems, I/O & networking, testing infra) was previously included here but has been removed pending regeneration against the current `redis-py`/`websockets`/`kubernetes_asyncio` rescope; a per-repo/per-category table computed on the corrected scope may be added as an appendix in a future revision if useful.*

The two database/cache-driver repos flagged in an earlier pass as scan-scope risks, `sqlalchemy` and `elasticsearch-py`, have since been rescanned restricted to their actual async submodules (`lib/sqlalchemy/ext/asyncio` and async dialect files; `elasticsearch/_async`), per the scan-scope note in Section 6. `elasticsearch-py`'s destructive share dropped from 10.5% (unscoped, 284 files) to 0.32% (scoped, 47 files) — confirming the earlier concern was real: most of what the unscoped scan had flagged as destructive was sync-code noise unrelated to asyncio interference. `sqlalchemy`'s destructive share moved more modestly, from 37.7% (unscoped, 269 files) to 34.4% (scoped, 11 files). The corpus-wide headline figures above already reflect the scoped rescan.

Extended controlled interference — mediated plus read-only — natively covers **71.54%** of interactions in the corpus (3.13% + 68.41%), with no encoding-cost penalty (Proposition 1a). For the commutative-write category (3.28%), the companion formal document defines *trace-equivalence* — invariance of every intermediate observable value under reordering, not just the final state — and proves a sufficient condition (disjoint-key writes, e.g. independent dictionary insertions, with no aggregate read before all writes complete) under which such writes get the same zero-cost encoding treatment as SNF-5. The definition is deliberately built to exclude counter increments: a counter's intermediate value is observable and order-dependent even though its final sum is not, so it fails the sufficient condition by construction. This is a proved partial result, not a full characterization — the corpus's 3.28% bucket was identified by a syntactic proxy during scanning and has not yet been re-checked against the precise condition.

The destructive write-conflict category (25.18%) is not a monolithic hazard: exactly this category is where Lemma 2a and Claim 2 break (Section 6), consistent with the failed write-conflict extension attempt discussed there. We specify a conservative static checker (companion document, Definitions 12, Proposition 11) that classifies each variable as mediated, read-only, checker-cleared (trace-equivalent), or flagged, with a proof that the classification never mislabels an unsafe variable as safe. This lets a verifier built on SNF fall back to general-purpose verification on flagged variables instead of silently applying an unsound encoding — a practical-enforcement contribution, distinct from and weaker than proving havoc injection itself sound. For the flagged variables the checker identifies, we additionally show (companion document, Proposition 12) that composing havoc injection with $\text{Enc}(P)$ is sound and quantifier-free whenever the flagged variable does not gate a branch (a syntactically checkable condition, Definition 13); when it does gate a branch, we further split the case (companion document, Definition 15, Proposition 13, tightened by 13a–13b) into finite, statically-enumerable branch structure — now proved sound via a path-sensitive encoding — versus a variable gating a genuinely unbounded loop, which remains open. Re-running the corpus with this split (`branch_check.py`, extended for Definition 15) shows: 63.79% of the flagged bucket is flagged-control, versus 36.21% flagged-data (9.1% and 16.1% of the whole corpus respectively). Within flagged-control, a syntactic While-test proxy for "unbounded loop" classifies 92.24% (58.84% of the whole flagged bucket) as satisfying the Bounded-Path Assumption — now covered by Proposition 13 — and 7.76% (4.95% of the flagged bucket) as unbounded-loop-gated and still open. This proxy has not been checked against Definition 15's precise semantic condition (it can misclassify an unboundedly-iterating `for` loop as bounded, and does not check guard exhaustiveness), so these percentages should be read as an upper bound on Proposition 13's practical coverage rather than a verified count — the same caveat already stated for Proposition 10's corpus overlap. The classification heuristic is deliberately conservative, so 25.18% is an upper bound on genuine data-flow risk in the corpus, not a measured incidence of bugs. A running-aggregate check of the pooled destructive-write rate across six repository orderings shows this figure has largely stabilized at N=33 (tail standard deviation 0.4–1.1 percentage points), a substantial improvement over the ~5-point swing observed in the last repositories added at N=22, though not yet perfect convergence.

Taken together, extended controlled interference natively resolves just over seven-tenths of real-world asyncio sharing; a proved sufficient condition extends zero-cost coverage to a well-scoped slice of the commutative-write bucket (exact overlap with the corpus's 3.28% not yet measured); and the remaining destructive-write bucket is safely detectable, even though verifying it is not yet possible.

### 5.7 Real-World Validation: `asyncpg.Pool` Connection Checkout

Sections 5.3–5.5 validate $\text{Enc}(P)$ on seven hand-written microbenchmarks (b1–b7) and synthetic PHP pigeonhole proxies. Neither is code anyone other than us wrote to solve a real problem. This subsection applies the same encoding methodology, by hand, to a real production code path from the corpus: `asyncpg`'s connection-pool checkout logic (`asyncpg/pool.py`, `PoolConnectionHolder._release` and `Pool._acquire`/`_acquire_impl`, MagicStack/asyncpg, inspected at commit-level source), one of the 33 repositories in the Section 5.6 corpus.

**The real code and a real scope question.** `asyncpg.Pool` mediates connection checkout exclusively through `self._queue`, but — unlike the paper's own `asyncio.Queue` examples — this queue is an `asyncio.LifoQueue`. We confirmed directly against CPython 3.12.3 source that `LifoQueue` overrides only `_init`, `_get`, and `_put` (which item is dequeued); the waiter-registration and wakeup machinery (`_getters`, `_putters`, `_wakeup_next`) that SNF-4's witness is about is inherited unchanged from `Queue`. This is a real extension of scope the theorem's statement does not make explicit: SNF-4 covers any `Queue` subclass that does not override `_wakeup_next`, including `LifoQueue`, and `asyncpg`'s pool is production evidence this distinction matters — a reviewer checking only "does this use `asyncio.Queue`" would have missed that the theorem's coverage extends here only because the overridden methods happen to be the ones SNF-4 does not depend on.

**Scenario and property.** We model two coroutines $A, B$ concurrently calling `pool.acquire()` (`Pool._acquire_impl`, line 879: `await self._queue.get()`) against a pool with one checked-in connection holder, and a third coroutine $R$ performing a release elsewhere in the application (`PoolConnectionHolder._release`, line 325: `self._pool._queue.put_nowait(self)`), making a second holder available shortly after. The safety property is the one an actual connection pool must guarantee: **no two acquirers ever receive the same holder object.** This is exactly extended controlled interference — all shared access to holder identity is mediated through `pool._queue` and nothing else.

**Structured encoding, hand-derived and checked in Z3.** We enumerated all $3! = 6$ FIFO-consistent dispatch orderings of $\{A, B, R\}$ and derived each disjunct by direct simulation of the real `get()`/`put_nowait()` control flow (including the `while self.empty(): ... await getter` suspend/resume path and `_wakeup_next`'s single-`popleft()` semantics) rather than by guessing an outcome. Asserting $\text{Enc}(P) \wedge (\text{holder}_A = \text{holder}_B)$ — the negation of the safety property — returns **unsat**: no feasible ordering of the real checkout logic ever produces a double-checkout, verified structurally across all six orderings rather than tested on a sample of them.

**A correctness-gap analog on real code.** To test whether Section 5.2's correctness-gap phenomenon appears outside our own benchmarks, we constructed a naive encoding embodying a specific, plausible modeling mistake: conflating `Queue.put_nowait`'s wakeup discipline (`_wakeup_next` resolves *exactly one* waiter via `popleft()`) with `Event`-style broadcast wakeup (`set()` resolves *all* waiters) — an easy conflation for anyone hand-encoding pool logic without checking CPython source, and asyncpg's own codebase uses both Queue- and Event/Lock-flavored primitives elsewhere. Under this naive model, the same double-checkout query — two blocked acquirers, one connection released — returns **sat**: the naive encoding claims a real connection-pool double-checkout is possible, when the actual CPython `asyncio.Queue` implementation makes it structurally impossible. This is a correctness gap on a real, production checkout path, not a synthetic one. The automated pipeline described below reproduces the identical mechanism — the same naive/structured divergence on `Pool._queue` — but only under an explicit, disclosed assumption about how `Pool.acquire()` is called; see below for exactly what that assumption is and why it's needed, rather than treating the two results as independent confirmations of the same unconditional fact.

**Automated confirmation and corpus-scale extension (`snf-pipeline`).** We built a pattern-based AST extractor and Z3 encoder (`ir.py`/`extractor.py`/`encoder.py`/`pipeline.py`) that derives both naive and structured encodings for `asyncio.Lock`/`Queue`/`Event` mutual-exclusion and broadcast-wake properties directly from source, with no manual modeling step, and a separate check for `wait_for`-timeout/cancellation races (walking the call graph up to 5 hops, same-file only, to locate cleanup handlers, including a broadened cleanup-verb list and a low-confidence fuzzy hint for calls that look cleanup-shaped but aren't an exact match). Run against the full Section 5.6 corpus (114 check rows: 70 sync-object checks, 44 `wait_for` sites): **22 of 70 sync-object checks show the naive/structured gap pattern** (`naive=sat, structured=unsat`), 44 show no gap (both `unsat`), and **4 — all `EVENT`-kind checks** (`aiohttp`, `aiosonic`, `sanic` ×2) — return `sat` under *both* encodings. This third outcome is neither a demonstrated gap nor a clean pass: it means the structured encoding did not rule out the checked violation either, which could reflect a genuine issue in the target code, a real limitation specific to the structured `EVENT` encoder's handling of that usage pattern, or a checked property that is not actually a true invariant of the intended usage — all `EVENT`-typed suggests a systematic rather than coincidental cause, and these four are flagged for manual triage rather than folded into either the "gap" or "no gap" counts. Of the 44 `wait_for` sites, 23 are cross-file/cross-module and out of this pattern-based tool's reach by design, 8 are same-file sites where no cleanup handler was found in any callee (the closest thing to genuine findings the tool can surface, and worth a manual pass before citing as bugs rather than flags), 10 are handled (9 locally, 1 via same-file call-chain resolution), and 3 have an unresolvable callee name. Every unresolved or flagged site is a triage signal, not a confirmed finding, by explicit design of the tool. The pipeline separately detects (but does not fix) a related risk: two unrelated classes defining a method with the same bare name, which the encoder's site-counting could merge into one bucket since coroutine identity is tracked by bare name, not class-qualified, for the same reason a full fix conflicts with the tool's own call-chain and multiplicity-propagation machinery (both of which must work by bare name, since `self.foo()` gives no class information in the AST). After filtering out near-universal, sync-irrelevant collisions (`__init__` and similar dunders, which collide in almost any multi-class file and essentially never touch a contended object), 12 actionable collisions remain across the corpus, each individually inspectable rather than presented as a single opaque risk count.

Two of the tool's own bugs were caught and fixed during this corpus run, and are reported here rather than silently corrected, since they materially affected an earlier version of this section's numbers and of the asyncpg finding specifically. First, nested `async def`s (the common shape for a benchmark's or a class's worker/task function, including asyncpg's own `_acquire_impl`, nested inside `_acquire`) were being visited twice by the extraction driver, silently doubling every synchronization operation inside them; this fabricated an apparent 2-waiter contention on `Pool._queue` out of what was actually one static call site counted twice, producing a false "gap reproduced" result. Second, a scope-tracking bug caused a nested function's own statements to be (coincidentally, harmlessly) resolved against its enclosing function's local variables, while the *enclosing* function's own direct statements were not — meaning a bare call in the outer function's own body that touched the same shared object (e.g. `event.set()` in the same function that declared `event = asyncio.Event()`) could silently fail to resolve, hiding a real gap rather than fabricating one. Both are fixed and covered by regression tests; the numbers above reflect the corrected tool.

Task-spawn multiplicity is derived from source wherever the same file spawns a coroutine as more than one concurrent task (`asyncio.create_task`/`asyncio.gather`/a spawning loop or comprehension) — this recovers the naive/structured gap for benchmark-style code whose worker functions are visibly spawned N times in the same function that declares the shared object. It does **not**, by itself, recover the asyncpg finding: `_acquire_impl`'s queue operations are visible in `pool.py`, but nothing in that file shows `_acquire_impl` being spawned concurrently — the actual concurrency comes from external, unknown callers of the public `Pool.acquire()` API, which is invisible to a single-file analysis. The pipeline supports an explicit, opt-in `--assume-public-concurrent` flag for exactly this shape: it treats a non-underscore-prefixed coroutine (dunder methods such as `__aenter__` count as public) as callable concurrently by at least two unknown external callers, propagates that assumption through the same-file call chain to whichever inner function actually touches the shared object, and prints which coroutines received multiplicity this way, kept in a separate, clearly labeled category from source-detected multiplicity. Only *with* this flag does the pipeline reproduce `naive=sat, structured=unsat` on `Pool._queue`; the unmodified default run — extraction with no assumption beyond what `pool.py`'s own source shows — returns `unsat/unsat`, correctly reflecting that a single file cannot, on its own, prove how many concurrent callers a public method has. We report both figures rather than only the one that matches the hand-encoded case study: the default run is the honest floor of what static single-file analysis can claim; the flagged run is what it can claim under a stated, auditable assumption about external usage, which is the same assumption the original hand-encoded case study made implicitly when it posited "two blocked acquirers" without deriving that number from `pool.py` itself.

**Scope of this validation, updated.** Applied specifically to `asyncpg`'s `pool.py`, the pipeline confirms the same naive/structured divergence found in the hand-encoding above, but only when explicitly told to assume `Pool.acquire()` may be called concurrently by external callers (`--assume-public-concurrent`) — without that flag, the default extraction of `pool.py` alone correctly returns `unsat/unsat`, since nothing in the file itself proves more than one caller ever invokes it at once. The pipeline additionally resolves the `wait_for`-timeout site at line 898 (`return await compat.wait_for(_acquire_impl(), timeout=timeout)`) regardless of that flag: both naive and structured return `unsat` there, with the tool's call-chain resolution correctly tracing into `_acquire_impl` and recognizing its `except (Exception, asyncio.CancelledError):` block (`self._queue.put_nowait(ch)`, line 883) as the cancellation-cleanup handler — so both the timeout path and the requeue-on-cancellation branch, previously stated as uncovered by the hand-encoded version, are now automatedly checked and found sound. This is not a clean bill of health for the whole file, though: the pipeline separately flags `release()` at line 228 (`structured=sat`, callee `_wait_for_cancellation` not resolvable — cross-file, needs manual check) as a genuinely open, unresolved item, distinct from and outside the original case study's scope. The core mutual-exclusion check remains bounded to one scenario ($n=3$ coroutines, one exception-free interleaving pattern) — the pipeline checks the same property the hand-encoding checked, not a more exhaustive one — but it is no longer a single hand-built example: it is one of 70 real-world sync-object checks the same tool performs mechanically across the corpus, on code from a real, widely-used library rather than on code written to exercise the theorem. What the automated version adds beyond confirmation is precision about *what kind of fact* the original hand-encoding's "two blocked acquirers" assumption actually was: not something derivable from `pool.py` alone, but an assumption about external usage that the pipeline now makes explicit and auditable rather than implicit.

## 6. Limitations

**Extended controlled interference.** The theorem applies only to programs satisfying extended controlled interference: coroutines with disjoint local variable sets where all write-write and write-read shared state passes through `asyncio.Queue`, `asyncio.Lock`, or `asyncio.Event`, plus unmediated read-only sharing (SNF-5, Proposition 1a). Programs with unmediated shared *writes* — a shared counter read by one coroutine and incremented by another without a Lock, a boolean flag set and polled across coroutines, a shared list appended to by multiple tasks — are outside the theorem's scope. This is a real restriction, not merely a boundary condition, because unmediated shared writes are precisely where the interesting concurrency bugs live. The corpus study (Section 5.6) shows this residual scope is 28.46% of real-world interactions across 33 repos (3.28% commutative-looking, 25.18% destructive), down from the full unmediated fraction now that SNF-5 is proved; the theorem is strongest where the verification problem is easiest and silent where it is hardest.

**Commutative writes — sufficient condition proved, not a full characterization.** The companion formal document defines trace-equivalence (invariance of every observable intermediate value, not just final state) and proves a sufficient condition — disjoint-key writes with no aggregate read before completion — under which such writes collapse to the same zero-cost encoding treatment as SNF-5 (Proposition 10). This correctly excludes counter increments, whose intermediate values are order-dependent even though their final sum is not. What remains open: whether a broader class of trace-equivalent writes exists beyond disjoint-key writes, and whether the corpus's 3.28% commutative-looking bucket (Section 5.6) actually satisfies this condition — it was identified by a syntactic proxy during scanning, not re-verified against Proposition 10 directly.

**Destructive write-conflicts — detectable, not yet verifiable.** The remaining 25.18% of corpus interactions are destructive write-conflicts, concentrated in database-driver repositories (Section 5.6), and are exactly the case shown below to break Lemma 2a and Claim 2. We do not claim to verify these in full. The companion document specifies a conservative static checker (Definitions 12, Proposition 11) that soundly detects when a variable falls outside the theorem's scope, so a verifier can fall back to general-purpose verification rather than silently apply an unsound encoding. Havoc-variable injection is the natural technique for regaining soundness on flagged variables; the companion document proves this composition sound and quantifier-free for flagged variables that do not gate a branch (Proposition 12). For branch-gating flagged variables, the companion document further splits the case (Definition 15, Proposition 13, tightened by Propositions 13a–13b): finite, statically-enumerable branch structure is now covered by a path-sensitive generalization of $\text{Enc}(P)$, while a flagged variable gating a genuinely unbounded loop remains fully open. Checking the corpus's 25.18% against this split shows: 36.21% of the flagged bucket is flagged-data (Proposition 12 applies), and 63.79% is flagged-control. Within flagged-control, a syntactic proxy measurement (`branch_check.py`) finds 92.24% satisfy the Bounded-Path Assumption (Proposition 13 applies) and 7.76% are unbounded-loop-gated and remain open — most real-world unsynchronized shared writes in this corpus gate a branch downstream, but the large majority of those branches are finite and now soundly handled, not the harder unbounded case the theorem still cannot reach. The proxy has not been checked against Definition 15's precise condition, so this split is an upper bound on Proposition 13's coverage, not a verified count.

**Database/cache-driver scan-scope note (resolved).** Two of the 12 database/cache-driver repos in the corpus study, `sqlalchemy` and `elasticsearch-py`, are large monorepos containing both sync and async code paths; an unscoped scan does not exclude sync-only modules, folding in a large volume of interactions unrelated to asyncio interference. We re-ran the scan for both repos restricted to their actual async submodules (`lib/sqlalchemy/ext/asyncio` and async dialect files for `sqlalchemy`; `elasticsearch/_async` for `elasticsearch-py`). The concern was substantiated: `elasticsearch-py`'s destructive share dropped from 10.5% (unscoped, 284 files) to 0.32% (scoped, 47 files), confirming most of what the unscoped scan flagged as destructive was sync-code noise. `sqlalchemy`'s destructive share moved more modestly, from 37.7% (unscoped, 269 files) to 34.4% (scoped, 11 files). All Section 5.6 figures reported in this paper reflect the scoped rescan; the database/cache-driver category mean rose slightly as a result (30.6% → 31.4%), since removing `elasticsearch-py`'s large below-average-destructiveness sync-code volume pulls the category's remaining average up rather than down.

**Scan-scope audit extended corpus-wide (partial — 3 of 26 candidates resolved).** The two-repo check above was manual and ad hoc; we subsequently built a direct measurement (`scope_contamination_check.py`) rather than relying on sync-file percentage as a proxy for contamination, since a high sync-only file share does not by itself imply the per-repo `(kind, name)` string bucketing (Definitions in the companion document's Limitations) is actually being poisoned — a repo full of sync-only test/tooling files with no shared attribute names in common with its async code is unaffected regardless of the percentage. Running this check against all 33 corpus repos found 17 with at least one direct, named collision between a sync-only file's write and an async-side read-only candidate of the same `(kind, name)`. These split into two categories requiring different treatment:

- **Genuine duplicate sync/async implementations (directory-scoping fixes, same mechanism as `sqlalchemy`/`elasticsearch-py`):** `redis-py` ships a classic sync client (`redis/client.py`) alongside a parallel async client (`redis/asyncio/`) with intentionally mirrored attribute names; rescoping to `redis/asyncio` dropped its collision count from 75 to 1 (of the 17 flagged repos, this was by far the largest single contributor). `websockets` ships a genuine threading-based synchronous client (`src/websockets/sync`) in addition to its asyncio implementation, plus a Trio backend (`src/websockets/trio`, already excluded from this study's scheduler-level Consistency Interface statistics for unrelated reasons — excluding it here is consistent with that earlier decision, not a new one); excluding both dropped its file count scanned from 73 to 62 and its collision count from 18 to 8. `kubernetes_asyncio` is structurally different — 858 of its original 896 files are auto-generated OpenAPI model/DTO classes (`kubernetes_asyncio/client/models`, e.g. `V1Pod`, `V1Service`) with plain `__init__` attribute assignments and no async methods at all; these are not "programs with interference" in any sense relevant to this study, and were the direct source of most of that repo's original collision evidence (`self.kind`, `self.api_version`, and similar generated-boilerplate names). Excluding this directory (plus its test directory) dropped its file count scanned from 896 to 128 and its collision count from 10 to 2. None of the three shows any overlap with the thread-offloading measurement (Section 6, `thread_overlap_check.py`) either before or after rescoping — 0 overlap interactions each — so the rescope affects only the destructive-write contamination figures, not the thread-disjointness numbers.
- **Generic attribute-name collisions across unrelated classes within an inherently async-only package (not fixable by directory scoping):** the remaining 14 flagged repos (`aiohttp`, `tortoise-orm`, `faust`, `sanic`, `aiokafka`, `asyncz`, `taskiq`, `motor`, `asyncpg`, `apscheduler`, `databases`, `quart`, `aiobotocore`, `httpx`) show collisions on common attribute names (`self.app`, `self.args`, `self.headers`, `self.request`, `self.config`) that recur across genuinely unrelated classes within a single async-only codebase, not a duplicate sync implementation. There is no sync subtree to exclude here; this is `refine.py`'s own documented per-repo string-bucketing limitation manifesting concretely, and fixing it requires per-class (not per-repo) name disambiguation — a materially larger methodological change than directory scoping, and an open item rather than a resolved one. Collision counts range from 1 (`quart`, `databases`, `apscheduler`, `aiobotocore`) to 25 (`aiohttp`).

Corpus-wide figures in this paper reflect the `redis-py`/`websockets`/`kubernetes_asyncio` rescopes — confirmed directly, via a rerun of `scope_contamination_check.py` after patching it to apply `REPO_ASYNC_EXCLUDE` (previously this script only supported `REPO_ASYNC_SCOPE` include-lists, so it had not itself reflected the `websockets`/`kubernetes_asyncio` exclude-based fixes even though `scan.py`'s own corpus numbers already did), and via the file-count changes reported above. The resulting shift in the corpus-wide destructive-write figure (26.63% → 25.18%) is consistent with removing contamination rather than an artifact of a different measurement. A per-repo/per-category destructive-rate breakdown (Section 5.6) has been removed from this revision pending regeneration against the new scope, rather than left in a stale state alongside the corrected corpus-wide figures; it may be added as an appendix in a future revision. The 14 Group B repos remain a genuine, quantified limitation of the per-repo bucketing approach rather than a to-do item resolved by more scoping.

**SNF's contribution is structural, not stability-differential.** The non-SNF QF\_LIA baseline experiment (Section 5.4) establishes that quantifier elimination alone closes the stability gap. SNF's value is that it provides a principled, structure-grounded method for deriving a correct and complete QF\_LIA encoding — one a verifier author can apply systematically from program structure rather than through mechanical QE, which collapses for non-satisfiable pigeonhole-style constraints and requires additional soundness arguments in general. The theorem proves correctness and completeness; stability is a consequence of quantifier-freedom, not of SNF specifically.

**CPython version specificity — checked across 3.9–3.13.** The SNF witnesses are verified directly against CPython 3.12.3 with the default task factory. `asyncio.eager_task_factory` is no longer a version caveat (resolved via Proposition 8′, above). For general version transfer, the companion document's Corollary 9a routes the question through the Consistency Interface already used for `uvloop` and Trio: CPython's own asyncio implementation is itself a scheduler in the sense the interface quantifies over, so checking I-1–I-4 against a version's `base_events.py`, `tasks.py`, `locks.py`, and `queues.py` is sufficient, by Proposition 9, to inherit SNF for that version. We ran this check (`cpython_version_diff.py`) against CPython 3.9.19, 3.10.14, 3.11.9, 3.12.3, and 3.13.0: all five show every I-1–I-4 witness pattern present under their 3.12-era names. This is a substring-presence check against the specific lines the Step 2 witnesses cite, not an independent semantic re-verification that the surrounding code still behaves as the pattern implies — a version could in principle retain an identifier while changing its behavior around it — but given the stability of asyncio's core dispatch loop since its introduction, a clean result across five releases spanning 3.9–3.13 is real positive evidence, not merely the absence of a check. The theorem's practical version scope is therefore CPython 3.9 through 3.13, structurally checked, with 3.12.3 as the version verified by direct hand-inspection (Proposition 1) and the others verified via Corollary 9a's interface reduction.

**Generalization beyond asyncio — scope of current evidence.** The Consistency Interface (Definition 9) is stated implementation-agnostically, but both schedulers checked against it so far — `uvloop` and Trio — are Python, and the passing case (`uvloop`) reuses CPython's own `Lock`/`Queue`/`Event` rather than reimplementing them. The interface has therefore been shown to discriminate a real pass from a real fail (Section 6), but that evidence is currently Python-only; it does not yet show the interface transfers across languages or concurrency models. Rust's `tokio` (multi-threaded work-stealing), Node.js's phase-based event loop, and Go's preemptive M:N goroutine scheduler are not checked here, and we do not treat this as a mere coverage gap: each has a specific published scheduling-model feature that we expect to violate a specific clause of (I-1)–(I-4). Work-stealing gives `tokio` no single global dispatch order, which we expect to fail (I-1) — determinism — the same clause Trio fails, though for an unrelated reason (design-intentional randomization vs. genuine concurrent dispatch). Node's ordered-phase structure complicates stating a single per-iteration dispatch bound, which we expect to strain (I-2). Go's asynchronous preemption at safe points breaks run-to-suspension execution, which we expect to fail (I-3). This is stated as the specific next target for source-level checking, in the same style already used for `uvloop` and Trio, not as a claim already established for these three schedulers.

**`asyncio.eager_task_factory` — first-step split, resolved.** `asyncio.eager_task_factory` alters the dispatch model in ways that break three of the four SNF conditions for a task's first step. Under eager execution, `Task.__step` is called synchronously during `create_task` rather than being scheduled onto `_ready`. This breaks SNF-1 (the task's first step does not go through `_run_once`'s FIFO queue), SNF-2 (the bound $n_{todo} \leq |C|$ is computed before the eager step occurs, so an eagerly-started task's first step is not counted), and SNF-3 (the first step runs inside the creating coroutine's own atomic segment, not in its own `__step` invocation). The companion formal document proves this as a case split: Proposition 7 shows a task's dispatch from its first suspension onward satisfies SNF-1–SNF-4 exactly as in the non-eager case, and Proposition 8 shows the Normal Form Reduction Lemma applies to the resulting restricted trace *provided* the eager first step itself touches no shared state (Definition 8). Proposition 8′ then removes that restriction: folding the eager step's post-constraints into its creator's dispatch step (a Definition 9 bookkeeping extension, not a new atomicity argument, since SNF-3's atomicity already covers the whole nested segment) preserves quantifier-freedom by simple syntactic closure and preserves the Claim 2 bijection because the eager step contributes no new scheduling degree of freedom; any unmediated shared-state access the eager step performs is classified by the existing static checker using the identical criteria already applied to every other coroutine. The theorem now covers `eager_task_factory` with no eager-specific restriction — an unmediated access during the eager step is exactly as open (or closed) as the same access would be for any ordinary coroutine, which is the existing flagged-control gap, not a new one. Separately, a source-wide scan (`eager_factory_check.py`) for any reference to `eager_task_factory` — literal usage, imports, or as an argument to `set_task_factory`/`Runner` — across all 33 corpus repositories found **zero**. Given that `eager_task_factory` is on a trajectory to become CPython's default, closing this gap ahead of that shift was worth doing regardless of current incidence.

**CPython free-threading (PEP 703, 3.13+).** CPython 3.13 introduces an experimental no-GIL mode. SNF-3's scheduling-level atomicity claim — no other coroutine is dispatched during $c_i$'s step — holds under free-threading because asyncio's event loop remains single-threaded by design. What free-threading threatens is a stronger property the paper does not claim: atomicity of individual Python object mutations within a coroutine's step against concurrent modification by OS-level threads. Programs that use `run_in_executor` or explicit threading alongside asyncio can have thread-level interference within a coroutine's step that SNF-3 does not protect against. For programs that use only async/await with no explicit threading, SNF-3's invariant is preserved under no-GIL; for programs mixing asyncio with threads, SNF-3 alone does not cover the interaction — the Thread-Disjointness Condition below closes this gap.

This carve-out is not a rare edge case. We checked six repositories from the Section 5.6 corpus (`aiohttp`, `asyncpg`, `aiomysql`, `quart`, `sanic`, `websockets`) for `run_in_executor`, `ThreadPoolExecutor`, or explicit `threading.Thread` usage and found it in 0.5%–8.5% of non-test source files per repo (7/82 in `aiohttp`, 2/26 in `asyncpg`), and, more importantly, concentrated in core rather than peripheral functionality: `aiohttp` uses `run_in_executor` for gzip/deflate compression, DNS-resolver fallback, and multipart file-upload handling (`web_request.py`); `asyncpg` uses it to offload blocking file I/O in its `COPY TO`/`COPY FROM` file-transfer methods (`connection.py`, confirmed directly against source: `_copy_out`/`_copy_in` call `self._loop.run_in_executor` for `open`, `read`, `write`, and `close`). We have since quantified the overlap directly (`thread_overlap_check.py`, corpus-wide, same-function granularity): of the 5,557 unmediated-destructive interactions, **43 (0.77%)** occur inside a function that also calls `run_in_executor` or constructs a `ThreadPoolExecutor`/`Thread`, concentrated almost entirely in `aiohttp` (31/626 destructive-unmediated interactions, 4.95%, in `post`/`prepare`/`compress`/`decompress`/`_sendfile_fallback`), `taskiq` (6/55, 10.91%), and `asyncz` (4/11, 36.36%). `asyncpg` shows **zero** same-function overlap under this measurement despite genuinely using `run_in_executor` in `_copy_out`/`_copy_in` — checked directly against source, this is not a detector miss: those two methods operate almost entirely on call-local variables (`f`, `path`, `writer`, `reader`), including a file handle captured in a per-call closure, not on `self.*` attributes or module-level shared state. The thread-vs-coroutine interference risk there is real (a concurrently running executor thread touching a local file handle while the coroutine's own step proceeds), but it is a different hazard than the `self.*`/global shared-state interactions this corpus study's classifier tracks, so it correctly does not appear in the destructive bucket at all — a real scope boundary between what this measurement covers and what it does not, rather than a contradiction. This measurement is a lower bound in the direction it does cover (same-function only, no call-graph analysis, constructor/`run_in_executor` detection only), so 0.77% should be read as "at least this much of the *tracked* corpus is affected," not as a complete accounting of every thread-related hazard in the corpus — but it is a real number in place of the previously unquantified caveat, and it says the tracked effect is real but small in aggregate, concentrated in a handful of repositories rather than uniformly distributed across the corpus.

**Thread-Disjointness Condition (TDC) — the blanket exclusion above is now a checkable side-condition, resolved in the companion document and confirmed empirically (below).** The preceding paragraphs treat "asyncio mixed with threading" as categorically outside the theorem. That is stronger than necessary: SNF-3 already guarantees no other *coroutine* interleaves during a step, and the only thing free-threading (or the GIL's absence) threatens is a thread touching state a coroutine's step also touches. The companion document (Definitions 17–18, Proposition 14) formalizes this as the Thread-Disjointness Condition: for every coroutine step that transitively submits work to `run_in_executor`/`ThreadPoolExecutor`/`Thread`, the submitted callable's reachable shared-state footprint must be disjoint from every concurrently-live coroutine's accesses, unless guarded by an OS-level primitive establishing a happens-before edge — mutual exclusion (`threading.Lock`/`RLock`) or a one-shot handoff signal (`threading.Event`/`Condition`/`Barrier`) awaited before the shared state is read (a different requirement than SNF-4's `asyncio.Lock` — TDC needs a happens-before edge for the specific accesses in question, not FIFO wakeup order). Proposition 14 proves that programs satisfying SNF-1–SNF-4, extended controlled interference, and TDC get the unmodified $\text{Enc}(P)$ with no additional encoding contribution from threaded code, because TDC's disjointness clause adds no new scheduling degree of freedom for Claim 2's bijection to account for. This converts "threading + asyncio ⇒ theorem does not apply" into the same graduated treatment already given to unmediated shared writes: a program failing TDC is flagged for general-purpose verification via the existing static checker (Definition 12), not silently mis-verified, and a program satisfying it is fully covered. We ran the corresponding call-graph checker across the full corpus; results and the one real-world case that motivated broadening the guard clause to include `Event` are given below.

We have since extended the development to mechanize Claim 2's bijection and Proposition 4's completeness argument for the abstract per-step permutation machine (`StepData`) that Lemma 2a and Claim 3 are already stated over, and Proposition 1a (SNF-5) directly. The full development is available at [repo link]. Formalizing the chained-rendezvous worked example (Section 4.1, Worked Example 2) surfaced a real gap in an earlier draft: the original version placed no yield point between the shared coroutine's get and put, so both rendezvous resolved within a single dispatch step, witnessing only Lemma 2a's within-step invariance and not Claim 3's cross-step composition. The current version corrects this by adding an explicit `await sleep(0)` between $c_1$'s get and put, splitting $r_1$'s resolution (step 1) from $r_2$'s (step 2); it is now a genuine witness of both Lemma 2a and Claim 3's inductive step. Lemma 2a's proof itself required no strengthening once isolated. One caveat carries through every result in Table 3, not only the newly-mechanized ones: mechanizing the *combinatorial* content of a claim is distinct from mechanizing the CPython-source-level content underneath it (that dispatch really is FIFO-consistent, that rendezvous really wake in FIFO order). That grounding remains Proposition 1's direct-inspection argument throughout, exactly as before — the mechanization closes the gap between "assumed bijection" and "proved bijection, given the same source-level facts every other result already depends on," not the gap between the formal model and CPython itself. ## 7. Future Work

**Bounded write-conflict: where SNF breaks and why.** The most important open direction is extending the theorem to programs with unmediated shared *writes*. We attempted to extend the Normal Form Reduction Lemma to the case of $k < n$ unsynchronized writers on a shared integer. The attempt fails at Lemma 2a: resolution-set invariance requires that whether a future completes depends only on the dispatching coroutine's own execution, not on the relative order of other coroutines in $Q_t$. For a shared write, this is false — if coroutine $c_i$ reads a variable written by $c_j$, whether $c_i$'s post-condition holds depends on whether $c_j$ executed before $c_i$ within the same dispatch step, which is exactly the scheduling choice Lemma 2a requires to be invariant. The bijection in Claim 2 also fails: distinct permutations of $Q_t$ produce distinct final states (different write orders produce different values), so the trace-to-sequence mapping is no longer onto. The corpus study (Section 5.6) shows this is not a hypothetical corner case: 25.18% of real-world interactions across 33 repos are destructive write-conflicts of exactly this kind, concentrated in database-driver code. Sound over-approximation via havoc-variable injection at the affected yield points is the natural next step (Section 6); this is now proved for the sub-case where the flagged variable does not gate a branch (Proposition 12, resolving 9.1% of the full corpus). For the majority sub-case — a flagged variable that gates a branch, 16.1% of the corpus — a further split (Proposition 13, tightened by 13a–13b) now soundly covers the finite-branch sub-case (a syntactic proxy measures this at 92.24% of flagged-control, 58.84% of the whole flagged bucket), leaving flagged-control variables gating a genuinely unbounded loop (7.76% of flagged-control, 4.95% of the flagged bucket, by the same proxy) as the remaining fully open case. More generally, the correct encoding for write-conflict programs would need to enumerate not just scheduling orders but also their effects on shared state — larger than $n!/2^r$ but potentially still finite and quantifier-free. Characterizing this structure precisely for the unbounded-loop sub-case is the next open problem.

**Commutative-write equivalence.** 3.28% of corpus interactions (Section 5.6) are writes that look reorderable without changing final state — independent dictionary insertions under distinct keys, for instance — but counter increments show the same intuition breaks down for operations whose intermediate states depend on order. A precise definition of commutativity over the abstract machine (Section 3.1), and a proof that it licenses collapsing the corresponding disjuncts of $F_t$ without losing completeness, is an open problem stated but not solved here.

**SNF-5 for read-only sharing — resolved.** SNF-5 (Section 3.1, Proposition 1a) is now a proved extension of controlled interference to unmediated read-only shared accesses: Lemma 2a and Claim 2's bijection are shown to be unaffected by such accesses. Benchmark b7 (read-only broadcast with no writer) validates the extension empirically (Section 5.3), and the corpus study (Section 5.6) shows it accounts for 68.41% of real-world interactions across 33 repos — the largest single category.

**Extending the mechanization.** The Lean development now covers Lemma 2a, Claim 3's inductive step, Claim 2's bijection, Proposition 4 (completeness), and Proposition 1a (SNF-5) — all as theorems, with no remaining free axiom on the combinatorial side. What is not, and cannot be, mechanized by this kind of development is the CPython-source-level grounding underneath every one of these results (Proposition 1's direct-inspection witnesses): that is a claim about a specific external artifact's behavior, not a mathematical statement, and stays a hand/source-inspection argument by construction. The remaining mechanization surface area is therefore Propositions 2–3 (size bound, quantifier-freedom), the main theorem itself (Step 5), and the havoc-injection and path-sensitive-encoding machinery (Propositions 10–13), none of which this development attempts.

**`eager_task_factory` — resolved.** Propositions 7–8 (companion document) prove the first-step/subsequent-step split for eager tasks whose first step touches no shared state; Proposition 8′ removes that restriction, extending Definition 9's per-step encoding to attribute the eager task's post-constraints to its creator's dispatch step. The theorem now covers `eager_task_factory` with no eager-specific restriction remaining — any unmediated shared-state access during the eager step is subject to exactly the same (already-existing) flagged-control gap as an ordinary coroutine's would be, not a new one. A corpus-wide scan for any reference to `eager_task_factory` across all 33 repositories found none, so this was closed ahead of the corpus needing it rather than in response to a measured gap.

**Thread-Disjointness Condition — call-graph re-measurement, run and confirmed by hand.** Proposition 14 (companion document) makes TDC a checkable side-condition rather than a blanket exclusion. We ran `tdc_callgraph_check.py` against the corpus, iterating through three successive fixes to the checker itself, each removing a distinct class of false positive: (1) an initial version flagged any function submitting thread/executor work regardless of whether it was a coroutine, which pulled in `websockets`' pure-synchronous `sync` submodule — a separate, non-asyncio threading-based client API outside SNF's scope entirely — producing 27 spurious hits; restricting candidates to genuine `async def` functions removed these entirely. (2) The next version computed the *entire enclosing coroutine's* shared-state footprint rather than the footprint of the specific callable handed to the thread, which flagged ordinary same-class coroutine methods sharing a `self.*` attribute (the normal, already-SNF-3-covered case) as if it were a thread hazard, inflating the count to 559; isolating the actual submitted callable's footprint per Definition 17 fixed this. (3) The corrected version still resolved bare method names (e.g. `get`, `close`) against any same-named function in the file, which matched call sites like `self.client.get` (an unrelated sync HTTP client object) or `f.close` (a local file handle) against the class's own unrelated same-named coroutine — the giveaway pattern being a coroutine appearing to "submit itself" to a thread (`get` submitting `get`, `_copy_in` submitting `close`), which is never a real pattern and always this bug. Restricting resolution to direct, unambiguous `self.<name>` targets removed 46 of the resulting 51 hits, correctly rerouting them to an "unresolved — external object, cannot determine without type info" bucket instead of asserting a false overlap.

The final run yields **three candidate overlaps corpus-wide**, all individually checked against source:

- `aiohttp`, `prepare`/`_make_response` sharing `self._path` with `_prepare_open_file` — confirmed benign. `_make_response` only reads `self._path`, which is set once in `FileResponse.__init__` and never mutated afterward.
- `aiohttp`, `compress`/`decompress` sharing `self._executor`/`self._max_sync_chunk_size` — confirmed benign; both are read-only configuration values fixed at construction.
- `asyncz`, `AsyncIOScheduler.start()`/`_init_new_loop` sharing `self.event_loop` with `shutdown` — flagged, but confirmed **not** a violation on inspection of the actual source: `_init_new_loop` writes `self.event_loop` inside the background thread and then signals a `threading.Event`; `start()` (both the sync and the `async def` variant, which does `await asyncio.to_thread(event.wait)`) does not return control until that signal fires, so any coroutine capable of reading `self.event_loop` can only run after the write has already happened-before it via the `Event`. This is real synchronization, just via a handoff primitive rather than mutual exclusion — motivating a revision to Definition 18 (companion document) to recognize any primitive establishing a happens-before edge (`Event`, `Condition`, `Barrier`, in addition to `Lock`/`RLock`), rather than mutual exclusion specifically.

**Net result: zero confirmed TDC violations across the corpus.** Combined with the already-small same-function overlap figure above (0.77%), this says asyncio+threading interaction in this corpus is both rare and, where present, already correctly synchronized by the libraries in question — not an unrecognized hazard the theorem was silently missing. The value Proposition 14 and TDC add is not a bug-finding result; it is replacing an unquantified blanket exclusion with a checked, near-empty violation count, using the same formal apparatus as the rest of the theorem rather than an ad hoc carve-out.

**CPython version conformance — checked.** Corollary 9a (companion document) reduces the pre-3.12/3.13+ version question to checking I-1–I-4 against each version's source, reusing the Consistency Interface below rather than re-deriving SNF-1–SNF-4 from scratch. We ran this check (`cpython_version_diff.py`) against CPython 3.9.19, 3.10.14, 3.11.9, 3.12.3, and 3.13.0, pulled from tagged releases on GitHub: all five show every I-1–I-4 witness pattern present under their 3.12-era names — the `ntodo`/`popleft()` FIFO snapshot (I-1/SNF-1) and its `range(ntodo)` bound (I-2/SNF-2) in `base_events.py`, `coro.send`/`coro.throw` in `tasks.py` (I-3/SNF-3), and the FIFO `_getters`/`_putters`/`_waiters` deque patterns in `queues.py`/`locks.py` (I-4/SNF-4). This is a substring-presence check against the specific lines the Step 2 witnesses cite, not an independent re-verification that the surrounding semantics are unchanged, but a clean result across five releases spanning four minor version boundaries is real positive evidence given how structurally stable this dispatch core has been. By Corollary 9a and Proposition 9, CPython 3.9 through 3.13's `asyncio.BaseEventLoop` (default task factory) inherits SNF and the main theorem for all five checked versions.

**uvloop satisfies the Consistency Interface (checked).** Rather than re-deriving SNF-1–SNF-4 from CPython internals for each new scheduler, the companion document factors the requirement into a four-property, implementation-agnostic Consistency Interface (Definitions 9, Proposition 9): any scheduler satisfying deterministic dispatch (I-1), bounded per-iteration dispatch (I-2), run-to-suspension atomicity (I-3), and statically-derivable wakeup order (I-4) inherits SNF and everything built on it. We checked all four properties directly against `uvloop` 0.22.1's Cython source (commit `0582f946`, 2026-05-04). I-1 and I-2 hold in `uvloop/loop.pyx`'s `_on_idle` dispatch routine, which captures `ntodo = len(self._ready)` before the dispatch loop and pops handles via `popleft()` in strict FIFO order for `i in range(ntodo)` — structurally identical to `BaseEventLoop._run_once`'s witness for SNF-1/SNF-2. I-3 holds because `handler._run()` executes synchronously inside libuv's single-threaded idle-handler callback, so no other coroutine's handle can run during a given handle's execution, mirroring the atomicity argument for `Task.__step`. I-4 holds because uvloop does not reimplement `asyncio.Lock`, `Queue`, or `Event` at all — a source-wide search finds no such classes anywhere in the uvloop package — so these remain CPython's stock pure-Python implementations with the same FIFO `_waiters`/`_getters`/`_putters` deques already verified for SNF-4, and their wakeup calls route through `loop.call_soon()` into the same FIFO `_ready` dispatch just confirmed for I-1/I-2. By Proposition 9, `uvloop` therefore inherits SNF and the main theorem without modification. This check is a source-level structural inspection, at the same evidentiary standard as the CPython witnesses for SNF-1–SNF-4 (Proposition 1, "proof by direct inspection"), not a mechanized or executable proof.

**Trio does not satisfy the Consistency Interface (checked, negative result).** We checked the same four properties against Trio (commit `865b7fc`, 2026-06-30). I-1 (deterministic dispatch) fails by explicit design: Trio's core scheduling loop (`src/trio/_core/_run.py`, `unrolled_run`) collects the current batch of runnable tasks and, outside of a Hypothesis-testing mode, executes `if _r.random() < 0.5: batch.reverse()` before dispatching — a 50% chance of reversing dispatch order on every scheduling pass. The source comment states the rationale directly: "we randomize the order of each batch to avoid assumptions about scheduling order sneaking in." This is not an incidental implementation detail but a stated design goal in direct opposition to SNF-1, which requires $S(Q)$ to be a deterministic total function. Since the Consistency Interface's four properties are conjunctive (Proposition 9 requires all four to inherit SNF), Trio fails to satisfy it at the first property and we did not proceed to check I-2–I-4. This is a genuine second data point for the Consistency Interface, not merely a second confirmation: it shows the interface correctly identifies a scheduler that should be *excluded* from SNF's guarantees, which is itself informative about the interface's discriminating power, and it independently corroborates the Section 5.6 corpus-study decision to exclude Trio from pooled statistics — that decision was originally made on the grounds that Trio's shared-state idioms don't map onto the `Sync(P)` mediation primitives our classifier detects; this scheduler-level check gives an orthogonal, stronger reason for the same exclusion. It also substantiates the concern that "an interface validated against a single scheduler instance isn't yet evidence the abstraction is the right one": with two data points, one confirming and one correctly rejecting, the interface's I-1 clause is doing real discriminating work rather than being trivially satisfied by every reasonable scheduler design.

**Scope of generalization: a scheduler class, not a Python-specific result.** The theory is not intended to be Python-specific, but the paper's evidence for generalization is currently limited to two checked instances plus the corpus study, and that scope should be stated plainly rather than left implicit. SNF itself is derived from CPython 3.12.3 source, but the Consistency Interface (Definitions 9, Proposition 9) exists precisely so that the target of the theorem is a *class* of schedulers — any scheduler satisfying deterministic dispatch (I-1), bounded per-iteration dispatch (I-2), run-to-suspension atomicity (I-3), and statically-derivable wakeup order (I-4) — rather than asyncio in particular. We have checked exactly two instances against this interface: `uvloop`, which inherits SNF (Proposition 9), and Trio, which fails at I-1 and is correctly excluded. This is real discriminating evidence, but it is evidence about two schedulers, both from the Python ecosystem. The corpus study (Section 5.6) is similarly scoped: its 33 repositories establish prevalence of extended controlled interference within asyncio code, not within concurrent code generally, and the paper should not be read as making a claim about non-Python schedulers' prevalence.

Runtimes outside Python are plausible candidates for a similar negative result to Trio's, for reasons that go beyond surface syntax. Work-stealing runtimes such as Rust's `tokio` (in its default multi-threaded configuration) dispatch across multiple OS threads with task-stealing between per-thread queues, which is not obviously a deterministic total function of a single ready-queue state and would need to be checked against I-1 and I-2 directly against source rather than assumed to fail or pass. Node.js's event loop is single-threaded and closer in spirit to asyncio, but its phase-based structure (timers, I/O callbacks, `setImmediate`, microtasks interleaved between phases) does not map onto a single FIFO `_ready` queue without first establishing what the analogous I-1–I-4 witnesses would even be. Go's goroutine scheduler is preemptive and work-stealing across M:N threads, which is a more severe departure from cooperative FIFO dispatch than Trio's randomized-batch design. None of these three have been checked against the Consistency Interface here; each would require the same kind of direct source inspection used for `uvloop` and Trio, and we would not be surprised if some or all fail at I-1 or I-3 for reasons specific to their scheduling model rather than incidental implementation choices. Checking them is future work, not a claim made by this paper.
